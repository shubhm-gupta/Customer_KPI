This study examines the antecedents and consequences of supply chain activities
and empirically tests many of the assumptions in the extant buyerâ€“supplier
relationship literature. To analyse the validity of the research model and the
hypotheses, data was gathered from 271 manufacturing organisations in South
Korea through the administration of structured questionnaires. The results
suggest that trust, interdependency, and rate of technological change are major
factors that influence the level of operational and strategic information sharing
and collaboration, and that the higher the degree of strategic information sharing
and collaboration is, the greater the level of effectiveness becomes. However,
there is no significant relationship between the length of relationship and
information sharing and collaboration. Research and practical implications of
these findings are discussed.

Keywords: supply chain dynamics; supply chain management; collaboration;
information sharing

1. Introduction

In todayâ€™s hypercompetitive environments in which competition is among supply chain
networks rather than individual firms, firms are confronted with the need to effectively
manage increasingly extending supply chain activities beyond the boundary of the firm
(Patnayakuni et al. 2006). The supply chain has become the central organising unit in
todayâ€™s global industries (Miles and Snow 2007). Since an individual firm alone is not able
to satisfy diverse customer needs, firms move towards closer and more integrated supply
chains (Bensaou 1997, Monczka et al. 1998, Lambert and Cooper 2000, Mentzer et al.
2000, Wisner 2003, Cousins and Menguc 2006). Resources invested in supply chain
relationships enable information sharing and collaboration between supply chain partners
(Patnayakuni et al. 2006, Pramatari 2007).

Supply chain antecedents are the factors that enhance or impede the implementation of
supply chain activities. A review of the supply chain and organisational behaviour
literature reveals that there are primarily three groups of antecedents of information
sharing and collaboration (Mentzer et al. 2001, Guimaraes et al. 2002): (1) relationship

2. Literature review

SCM research has focused on three categories: antecedents, activities, and, consequences.
Antecedents are the factors that enhance or impede the implementation of SCM activities.
A review of the logistics, marketing, and organisational behaviour literature reveals that
there are primarily three groups of antecedents of information sharing and collaboration:
(1) relationship characteristics (Morgan and Hunt 1994, Handfield and Bechtel 2002,
Kwon and Suh 2004, Rinehart et al. 2004, Chu and Fang 2006); (2) organisational
characteristics (McAfee et al. 2002, Pagell 2004); and (3) transactional characteristics
(Adewole 2005, Li et al. 2006). These antecedents serve as preconditions that need to be in
place in order for a firm to implement SCM with its suppliers and customers.

To achieve SCM objectives successfully, firms need to conduct a series of SCM
activities. A number of
studies viewed information sharing (i.e. exchange) and
collaboration as major SCM activities (Bensaou 1997, Monczka et al. 1998, Hill
and Scudder 2002, Moberg et al. 2002, Sahin and Robinson 2002, Lee and Lim 2003, Min
and Mentzer 2004, Morton et al. 2006, Krause et al. 2007). Information sharing and
collaboration have been identified and defined as either operational or strategic in many
studies (Mentzer et al. 2000, Moberg et al. 2002). To maximise SCMâ€™s full potential,
supply chain partners need both operational and strategic information sharing and
collaboration. Operational information sharing and collaboration are as-needed, shorter-
term activities for obtaining operational efficiency. Strategic information sharing and
collaboration are on-going, long-term inter-firm activities for achieving strategic goals,
which deliver value to customers and profitability to partners. In the following, we review
major studies in the relationship among antecedents, activities, and consequences.

2.1 Antecedents, activities, and performance

Mentzer et al. (2001) provided a theoretical framework that consists of supply chain
orientation (SCO), supply chain management (SCM), and business performance. They
suggested that
the SCO variables include credibility, commitment, organisational
compatibility, an agreement on SCM vision and key processes, mutual dependence,
leadership, and top management support. The set of SCM variables suggested include
integrated behaviour,
information sharing, mutual sharing of risks and rewards,
cooperation, agreed vision and goals, process integration, and long-term relationship.
Performance variables include product and services offering, profitability, timeliness, and
availability. In a follow-up empirical study, Min and Mentzer (2004) developed
measurement scales in accordance with their theoretical framework, and investigated
empirically the relationships among these variables.

Monczka et al. (1998) investigated factors from buying firmsâ€™ perspective that affect
the success of strategic supplier alliances. The critical antecedents associated with the
success of strategic supplier alliances include alliance attributes (commitment, trust and
moderation, and interdependency), communication behaviour (information quality and
participation, and information sharing), conflict resolution methods (mutual problem
solving, persuasion, unilateral reprimand, and external reliance), and the existence of
a formal sourcing procedure (supplier and products). The magnitude of the effect of these
antecedents on alliance success was assessed. They found that trust and moderation,
interdependency, information quality and participation, information sharing, and conflict
resolution methods are important factors, among others.

More recently, Li et al. (2006) developed five dimensions of SCM practice (strategic
supplier partnership, customer relationship,
level of information sharing, quality of
information sharing, and postponement) and tested the relationships between SCM
practices, competitive advantage, and organisational performance. The analysis of the
data collected from 196 organisations indicated that higher levels of SCM practice can lead
to enhanced competitive advantage and improved organisational performance.

3. Research model and hypotheses

This study proposes an integrative research model that consists of a set of SCM
antecedents, activities, and consequences, and empirically tests
the relationships
between these model elements using a field survey of manufacturing firms from various
industries.

While trying to keep the research model parsimonious, we selected the antecedents
under investigation based on three criteria: (1) the antecedents which are expected to affect
information sharing and collaboration, and have been considered in many previous
studies; (2) the antecedents which are expected to affect supply chain activities, but were
not widely studied; or (3) the antecedents which need further investigation because of
mixed results. The antecedents which are expected to affect supply chain activities and
have been considered in many previous studies are trust, commitment, interdependency,
top management support, and information quality (Bensaou 1997, Monczka et al. 1998,
Lee and Kim 1999, 2003, Mentzer et al. 2001, Moberg et al. 2002). The antecedents which
are expected to affect supply chain activities, but were not widely studied, include length of
relationship, cultural similarity, and goal compatibility (Bensaou 1997, Lee and Kim 1999,
Patnayakuni et al. 2006). Finally, the antecedents which need further investigation because
of mixed results include rate of technological change (Mendelson and Pillai 1998,
Guimaraes et al. 2002).

Our model proposes a one-tier suppliersâ€“manufacturers supply chain model. It would
be too complex and ineffective to investigate the three-stage supply chain activities among
supplier, manufacturers, and customers, because factors applicable for a supplierâ€“
manufacturer tier and a manufacturerâ€“customer tier are very different from each other.
Therefore, like most other researchers who limited their studies to the one-tier relationship
and performance, we focused on understanding the supply chain phenomenon in the
supplierâ€“manufacturer business settings. It is hoped that future research will develop the
three-stage supply chain model and investigate the activities and performance of the entire
supply chain.

The proposed research model, as shown in Figure 1, consists of three categories of
antecedents (relationship characteristics, organisational characteristics, and information/
technology characteristics), two categories of SCM activities (information sharing and
collaboration), and two categories of performance (efficiency and effectiveness).
The research model will examine the relationships among these model elements.

3.1 Trust

In this study, trust is defined as the extent to which firms believe that supply chain
partners will fulfill their responsibility to each other in good faith. Firms will share
information based on the belief that the supply chain partner will forego opportunistic
behaviour and use the shared information in a mutually beneficial way. A number of
studies suggest that trust is also an antecedent of inter-firm collaboration (Monczka
et al. 1998, Kwon and Suh 2004, Morton et al. 2006). This expectation leads to the
hypotheses:

H1a, b: There is a positive relationship between trust and operational/strategic information
sharing, respectively.
H1c, d: There is a positive relationship between trust and operational/strategic collaboration,
respectively.

3.2 Commitment

Commitment is defined as the extent to which supply chain partners will maintain and
strengthen their business relationship. Morgan and Hunt (1994) defined commitment as an
exchange partnerâ€™s belief that an ongoing relationship with another is so important as to
warrant maximum efforts at maintaining it. Firms need to invest a considerable amount of
money, time, and labour in developing information systems for information exchange and
may face a risk because of information exposure. Thus, a commitment is central to all of
the information exchange and collaboration between trading partners (Moberg et al. 2002,
Yeh 2005). Commitment
facilitates investments highly specific to the relationship.
These investments make it costlier and more difficult for supply chain partners to switch
to other firms, thus strengthening the motivation and incentives for greater collaboration
(Bensaou 1997). Hence:

H2a, b: There is a positive relationship between commitment and operational/strategic
information sharing, respectively.
H2c, d: There is a positive relationship between commitment and operational/strategic
collaboration, respectively.

3.3 Interdependency

Interdependency is defined as the extent to which supply chain partners believe that their
business relationship is necessary. As inter-organisational interdependency increases, the

need for coordination and collaboration also increases and information sharing

interdependency increases, collaborative activities will increase. Based on these findings,

H3a, b: There is a positive relationship between inter-organisational interdependency and
operational/strategic information sharing, respectively.
H3c, d: There is a positive relationship between inter-organisational interdependency and
operational/strategic collaboration, respectively.

3.4 Length of relationship

Length of relationship is defined as the period for which supply chain partners have
a business relationship. When supply chain partners have an on-going long-term business
relationship, they are more willing to exchange information necessary for business
transactions including information related to new product development, product cost, and
quality (Bensaou 1997). As the length of the relationship increases, information sharing
and collaborative activities will increase (Ganesan 1994). Hence:

H4a, b: There is a positive relationship between the length of relationship and operational/
strategic information sharing, respectively.
H4c, d: There is a positive relationship between the length of relationship and operational/
strategic collaboration, respectively.

3.7 Goal compatibility

Goal compatibility is defined as the extent to which supply chain partners have clear and
agreed-upon transactional goals. Compatible goals facilitate SCM policy integration
(Mentzer et al. 2001). Sharing common goals will guide cooperative activities between
partners and facilitate resource integration and utilisation (Wathne and Heide 2000).
If supply chain partners have compatible goals, they will understand partner activities
better and increase information sharing and collaboration. Hence:

H7a, b: There is a positive relationship between goal compatibility and operational/strategic
information sharing, respectively.
H7c, d: There is a positive relationship between goal compatibility and operational/strategic
collaboration, respectively.

3.8 Information quality

Information quality is defined as the value of the information shared by supply chain
partners. Managers may not use information from supply chain partners if they do not
have confidence in information quality, and furthermore will not provide their own
information to their partners. Supply chain partners must perceive accurate, current, and
reliable information from other firms in order to facilitate information sharing and
collaboration (Petersen et al. 2005). Hence:

H8a, b: There is a positive relationship between information quality and operational/strategic
information sharing, respectively.
H8c, d: There is a positive relationship between information quality and operational/strategic
collaboration, respectively.

3.9 Rate of technological change

Rate of technological change is defined as the rate of technological change of the products
of the supply chain partners. Because technological changes cannot be dealt with by an
individual firm, firms try to build a close business relationship to jointly develop new
technologies and products (Mentzer et al. 2000). The increase in the rate of technological
change leads to a greater degree of environmental uncertainty (Bensaou 1997, Guimaraes
et al. 2002). Mendelson and Pillai (1998) showed that, as the business environment
becomes more dynamic, the use of internal and external communication technologies and
the adoption of informational focus strategies increase. When the rate of technological
change is fast-paced, partners will facilitate information sharing and collaboration.
Hence, the following hypotheses:

H9a, b: There is a positive relationship between the rate of technological change and
operational/strategic information sharing, respectively.
H9c, d: There is a positive relationship between the rate of technological change and
operational/strategic collaboration, respectively.

3.11 Information sharing and performance

Efficiency is defined as the extent to which buyers save their resources from the supply
chain activities with sellers. Recently, supply chain efficiency has drawn much attention
from researchers (Sanders and Premus 2002, Ross and Droge 2004, Saeed et al. 2005).
Effectiveness refers to the extent to which buyers achieve their goals from the supply chain
activities with sellers. Information sharing between firms can solve information distortion
such as the bullwhip effect and reduce transaction costs (Simchi-Levi et al. 2003). When
firms share information that affects their business activities, they can accomplish tasks
more effectively by utilising the information systematically and coordinating their
activities

3.12 Collaboration and performance

Once firms develop a collaborative relationship, they can increase their organisational
efficiency by minimising unnecessary efforts or redundant activities (Chopra and Meindl
2004). Collaboration enables suppliers to achieve cost reduction and stable growth
through economy of scale, product quality, and learning effect. In turn, suppliers can
benefit buyers by providing products at a lower price (Monczka et al. 1998). Therefore,
collaboration helps supply chain partners achieve their business goals. Hence:

H12a, b: There is a positive relationship between operational/strategic collaboration and the
efficiency of buying firms, respectively.

Evaluation of discriminant validity of the measurement model was based on the
variance extracted test (Netemeyer et al. 1990). Table 4 shows the comparison of
squared correlation and variance extracted estimates. According to the variance
extracted test, the discriminant validity between two constructs is demonstrated if their
variance extracted estimates exceed the squared correlation coefficient between the
two constructs. Table 4 shows that the variance extracted estimates (diagonal values)
are all greater than the squared correlation coefficients (values below the diagonal line).
Our findings support the reliability and validity of the constructs and their indicator
variables.

To test the theoretical research model (see Figure 1), a path analysis with latent
variables was performed using the structural equation model (SEM) with the scales
averaged to form composites and a maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) method.
Our sample size of 271 subjects was adequate for testing the full SEM (with 55 measured
items). Table 5 summarises goodness of fit indices. In addition to the chi-square test
statistics, five fitness indices were used to evaluate the adequacy of the theoretical model.
The 2 test evaluated the discrepancy between the theoretical model and the collected data.
The smaller the 2 value, the better the model fit becomes. According to Carmines
and McIver (1981), 2 to degrees of freedom ratios (2/df) around 3.0 or less are
indicative of an acceptable model fit. The 2/df in this study is 1.760, an indication of an
acceptable fitness of our theoretical model with the data. Given that model 2/df was less
than 3.0, RMSEA was less than 0.8, NFI and IFI each exceeded 0.9, and GFI and AGFI
exceeded 0.9 and 0.8, respectively, the observed data demonstrated good fit with the
hypothesized model (Bentler and Bonnett 1980). Table 6 summarises the testing results
of the 48 hypotheses and Table 7 provides test statistics.

6. Discussion

One of the purposes of our study is to develop a research model and hypotheses on the
relationships between the variables. Results of our study show that trust has a significant
positive effect on operational and strategic information sharing as well as operational and
strategic collaboration, respectively. This indicates that as trust between supply chain
partners increases, the level of information sharing to support mutually beneficial business
activities increases. Similarly, as trust between supply chain partners increases, the level of
collaboration to reduce uncertainty and risk increases, out of the belief that collaboration
will lead to a mutual benefit.

Commitment had a significant positive effect on strategic information sharing and
strategic collaboration. However, commitment does not play a significant role in
motivating supply chain partners to utilise operational
information sharing and
operational collaboration. These results may be attributable to the fact that strategic
information sharing and strategic collaboration bring about considerable business risks
as well as benefits for partners. Therefore, implicit or explicit commitment of continued
future transactions will
lead to more strategic information sharing and strategic
collaboration because of reduced risks.

to engage in operational
information sharing, operational collaboration, strategic
information sharing, and strategic collaboration. The results suggest that inter-firm
interdependency increases information sharing and collaboration because individual firms

7. Conclusion

This study started with three main research questions: (1) what are the key antecedents and
consequences of supply chain activities?; (2) what are the effects of these antecedents on
supply chain activities?; and (3) how do supply chain activities affect buyersâ€™ supply chain
performance? To answer these questions, nine antecedents of supply chain activities,
four supply chain activities, and two consequences were identified and defined.
These antecedents and activities were linked to supply chain consequences by drawing
on related studies in supply chain management.

Results suggest that trust, interdependency, and rate of technological change are major
factors that influence the level of operational and strategic information sharing and
operational and strategic collaboration, and that the higher the degree of strategic

strategic information sharing. However, no significant relationship exists between the

7.1 Implication for researchers

information systems, marketing, and organisation theory,

First, by systematically integrating previous studies from the fields of operations
management,
this study
developed an integrative research model and contributed to the identification of the
causal relationships among supply chain antecedents, activities, and consequences.
Second, our study considered both information sharing and collaboration activities as
important mediating variables. Previous studies tended to focus on either information
sharing or collaboration and therefore could not analyse these variables at the same time.
Our study was able to investigate the individual effect of these variables on performance.
Third, previous studies did not investigate the relationships between the antecedents and
the operational and strategic activities and between these activities and the consequences.
In light of the limitations of the previous studies, our study analysed their relationships in
depth by distinguishing supply chain activities into operational and strategic activities and
supply chain consequence into efficiency and effectiveness.

7.2 Implication for practitioners

First, to facilitate information sharing and collaboration, supply chain partners need to
build mutual trust. The most fundamental factor that contributes to the formation of trust
is economic satisfaction. In addition, it is necessary to support partners via managerial
consulting and education and maintain transaction transparency as a way to build trust
via non-economic satisfaction. Second, to facilitate information sharing and collaboration,
supply chain partners need to increase interdependency. Buchanan (1992) suggested that
under an uncertain and dynamic environment a balance of power achieved through
mutual
interdependency helps constrain partnersâ€™ unfair power exercise and reduce
conflict and dissatisfaction. Third, before supply chain partners start business transac-
tions, they need to establish vision and goals together and communicate with each other.

7.3 Limitations of the study

As is typical
in many empirical studies, this study is not without limitations. First,
a number of important variables may not have been included in the research model that we
attempted to develop. Some factors would influence the performance variables. Examples
of such factors include size of suppliers (relative to buying organisation) and relative
power, number of suppliers, number and types of raw materials and products, nature and
complexity of production process, demand variability, etc. For instance, how cooperative

Second, focusing only on manufacturing firms may be another concern. To generalise
